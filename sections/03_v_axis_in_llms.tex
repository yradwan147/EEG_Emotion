\section{A Universal Valence Axis in Language Models}
\label{sec:vaxis-llms}

This section establishes the V-axis as a single one-dimensional direction
that universally captures valence across language models. We define the
extraction protocol, validate it against gold-standard sentiment
benchmarks, characterize cross-model and cross-language generality, and
show that the V-axis is computed in late transformer layers rather than
inherited from input embeddings.

\subsection{Extraction protocol}

Given a language model with hidden dimension $D$ and the FACED 9-emotion
taxonomy $\mathcal{C}=\{$Anger, Disgust, Fear, Sadness, Neutral,
Amusement, Inspiration, Joy, Tenderness$\}$, we author one short
emotion-evocative story per class (one sentence to one paragraph). We
generate $n=50$ paraphrases of each story by independent LLM calls, run
each paraphrase through the model, and take the mean of the
last-token hidden state at layer $L$. This yields nine class centroids
$c_1,\dots,c_9 \in \mathbb{R}^D$. We then run PCA on the
$9\!\times\!D$ centroid matrix and define the \textbf{V-axis} as the
unit vector along $\mathrm{PC}_1$, oriented so that the projection of
the Joy centroid onto it is positive. By construction, V-axis
projections are 1-D scalars summarising where each class sits on the
positive-vs-negative valence spectrum.

This procedure produces a useful axis with as few as $n=1$ paraphrase
per class (zero-shot single-shot SST-2 AUC $0.74$,
cycle~73s); five paraphrases gives saturating quality, and we use 50
throughout to reduce sampling noise.

\subsection{Universality across language models}
\label{sec:vaxis-llms-universality}

We extract the V-axis from 18 language models spanning three orders of
magnitude in scale (Qwen3.5 1.7B--72B, Llama-3 8B,
Llama-4-Scout-17B, Mistral-7B, OLMo-7B, Phi-2 2.7B, Pythia-1.4B,
BLOOM-560M, TinyLlama-1.1B, Gemma-27B, Gemma-4 31B). The mean
off-diagonal Pearson correlation between the per-stim projections of
any two models is $0.654$, with the strongest agreement among Qwen3.5
variants (Qwen3.5-1.7B vs Qwen3.5-14B: $r=0.94$, Qwen3.5-7B vs
Qwen3.5-14B: $r=0.96$). The behaviourally best-aligned models are
mid-size Qwen3.5; we observe an \emph{inverted-U} scaling law on
text benchmarks in which Qwen3.5-1.7B at layer~28 leads SST-2
zero-shot ($0.868$), with mid-to-large Qwen3.5
variants (3B--14B) tying on brain prediction
(Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain}).

\paragraph{Three regimes of cross-LLM agreement.}
The 18-LLM universality is not uniform: models cluster into three
regimes by per-stim correlation against behavioural valence, which
we read as evidence that universality requires \emph{sufficient
capacity to learn a clean valence concept}, not size per se. We
report the per-stimulus alignment to a behavioural valence reference
($n{=}28$ FACED stim, mean of $123$ subjects' valence ratings):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Top tier} (per-stim $r > 0.85$, $n=13$): Qwen3.5-14B
($r=0.964$), Qwen3.5-7B ($0.957$), Qwen3.5-3B ($0.947$),
Qwen3.5-1.7B ($0.938$), Qwen3.5-72B ($0.923$), Mistral-7B ($0.935$),
Qwen3.5-0.5B ($0.891$), Llama-3-8B, OLMo-7B, Llama-4-Scout, plus
three further Qwen3.5 variants. This is the \emph{universal manifold}.
\item \textbf{Middle tier} ($0.6 < r < 0.85$, $n=2$): Gemma-27B
($0.81$), Gemma-4-31B ($0.72$). Gemma-4 in particular is the
architectural anomaly we discuss separately below.
\item \textbf{Out-of-manifold} ($r < 0.4$, $n=3$): Pythia-1.4B
($0.28$), TinyLlama-1.1B ($0.13$), BLOOM-560M ($0.03$). These models
either failed to learn a clean valence concept or store it in a
non-late-layer representation.
\end{itemize}

The within-Qwen scaling is essentially flat ($r=0.249$, $p=0.519$,
$n=9$) once a model has joined the universal manifold:
universality is \emph{not} a size-monotonic phenomenon. The
inverted-U is real but small (Qwen3.5-1.7B 0.868 vs.\ Qwen3.5-7B
0.848 vs.\ Qwen3.5-32B 0.831 on SST-2 zero-shot); above
$\sim$1.7B parameters the recipe is essentially saturated.
Per-LLM brain-alignment ranks recur with this same three-tier
structure (Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain}, Figure~\ref{fig:llm-brain}).

The V-axis also recovers three industry-standard valence lexicons
zero-shot: NRC Emotion Lexicon \citep{mohammad2013nrc} valence
($|r|=0.79$), Hu \& Liu opinion lexicon \citep{hu2004mining}
($|r|=0.76$, $n=4{,}783$ words), and the Warriner et al.\ 13k word
ratings \citep{warriner2013norms} ($|r|=0.66$). We also exceed an SBERT
baseline at the same pipeline by $\Delta\,|r|\,\in\,[0.15,0.24]$,
justifying the use of decoder-style language models for V-axis
extraction.

\subsection{V-shape emergence: a late-layer phenomenon}
\label{sec:vshape}

The V-axis is not inherited from input embeddings; it is \emph{newly
computed} in late transformer layers. We sweep all 28 layers of
Qwen3.5-1.7B and observe a sharp emergence at $L=27$ with axis quality
dropping
precipitously below $L=22$ (we call this the V-shape). The cosine
between input-layer V-axis and final-layer V-axis is only $0.291$, with
mid-layer L14 at $0.067$ --- the late layer carries
fundamentally different information. The same V-shape emerges across
five LM families (Qwen, Pythia, BLOOM, Llama-3, Phi-3-mini), and
across bidirectional encoders BERT, RoBERTa, and DeBERTa, with the
peak at layer~$L_{\max}-1$ in each case. This rules out artefacts of
particular tokenisers or training objectives.

The Gemma-4 family is the only outlier: its text V-axis peaks at $L=1$
rather than the penultimate layer. Importantly, this anomaly is
\emph{not} reflected in the brain alignment (Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain}):
Gemma-4-E2B at $L=17$ predicts EEG at $r=0.819$, and Gemma-4-E4B at
$L=21$ at $r=0.714$. Universality is robust to this architectural
quirk.

\subsection{Crosslingual and cross-modal generality}

\paragraph{Multilingual transfer.}
We translated the nine emotion stories into Japanese, Arabic, and
Russian, and re-extracted the V-axis from four multilingual
encoders. Direct application of an English-centric causal LM
(Qwen3.5-1.7B) to non-English stories collapses to chance --- a clean
English-bias diagnostic. Switching to the multilingual mT0-base
encoder--decoder \citep{muennighoff2022mt0} fully recovers the
zero-shot SST-2 result and \emph{exceeds} the English baseline in
every language tested:

The mT0-base result rules out a "valence is English-specific
construct" objection: a 277M-parameter multilingual encoder--decoder
extracts a V-axis from non-English stories that beats the English
ceiling, suggesting the V-axis is genuinely a property of the LM's
pretraining-induced affect representation rather than an artefact of
English tokenisation or training data. Per-language V-axis cosines
are moderate ($r_{en\text{--}es}=0.47$, $r_{en\text{--}fr}=0.16$,
$r_{es\text{--}fr}=0.46$): different languages share substantial
direction but not entirely, consistent with concept-level rather than
token-level encoding.

\paragraph{Cross-modal generality (CLIP vision).}
The same protocol applied to nine emotion-evocative \emph{images}
via CLIP visual encoders \citep{radford2021clip} yields a V-axis
that ranks the OASIS image dataset \citep{kurdi2017oasis} on
valence at Pearson $r=0.869$ and on arousal at $r=0.803$. Both
\emph{beat} the supervised Ridge regressions trained on the OASIS
labels themselves (valence $0.836$, arousal $0.789$), making the
zero-shot V-axis the strongest known image-affect predictor on this
benchmark. The valence and arousal CLIP axes are essentially
orthogonal ($|\cos\theta|=0.013$), confirming clean dimensional
separation in vision space. The image-arousal success contrasts
sharply with the text-arousal failure (below), and we use this
asymmetry to delineate the recipe's scope.

\subsection{Compositionality and concept-library generality}
\label{sec:concept-library}

\paragraph{Compositional axis arithmetic.}
The V-axis is compositional. Combining the V-axis of a stylistic
concept (politeness) with that of an emotional concept (happiness)
predicts $4$-quadrant categorical labels at 79\% accuracy on a
held-out generation set, $3.16\times$ above chance, with uniform
per-cell precision in the $[0.68, 0.86]$ range. Subtractive
orthogonalisation (Gram--Schmidt removal of the valence component
from the politeness axis) cleanly separates the two dimensions:
happy-AUC $0.912 \to 0.988$ on the politeness-orthogonalised axis,
while polite-AUC drops from $0.793 \to 0.560$. The two axes therefore
behave as approximately linearly composable directions in the
late-layer residual stream, validated downstream on natural text
where the same composition predicts GoEmotions, SST-5, and Yelp
quadrants at 41.8\%--46.7\% (well above chance, below the
agent-authored 79\% — a documented scope refinement).

\paragraph{Recipe generality across 20 concepts.}
The same nine-prompt PCA recipe extends well beyond emotion. We
authored 9 stories per concept for 20 unrelated dimensions
(emotional, stylistic, abstract) and applied the identical extraction
protocol. \textbf{20 of 20 concepts reach binary AUC $> 0.65$ on
held-out test stories, and 17 of 20 reach AUC $\ge 0.95$}, with
eleven concepts hitting a perfect AUC $=1.00$.
Figure~\ref{fig:concept-library} reports the full per-concept results.

\paragraph{Cross-concept transfer matrix.}
We further measured what happens when we apply the V-axis of one
concept to a different concept's pole-vs-pole benchmark. The
$20\!\times\!20$ transfer matrix has self-AUC mean $0.97$ on the
diagonal and off-diagonal mean $0.83$, a $+0.13$ gap that confirms
the recipe extracts \emph{concept-specific} directions rather than a
single global affect axis: if every concept axis collapsed onto
valence, the off-diagonal would match the diagonal.
The most general source axes (high mean off-diagonal, indicating
concepts whose directions also classify other concepts) are
\textbf{sarcasm} ($0.89$), \textbf{fear} ($0.88$), \textbf{envy} and
\textbf{shame} ($0.87$) --- consistent with affect-laden concepts
recovering each other. The most specific source axes are
\textbf{decisiveness} ($0.78$) and \textbf{complexity} ($0.78$),
where the late-layer direction is more concept-isolated. The most
\emph{captured} concepts (high mean other-axis AUC) are
\textbf{urgency} ($0.97$), \textbf{fear} ($0.95$), and
\textbf{concreteness} ($0.94$); the most concept-specific (lowest
captured-by-others AUC) are \textbf{specificity} ($0.65$) and
\textbf{sarcasm} ($0.67$). Together this argues the recipe is a
\emph{family} of late-layer concept extractors, with valence one
member among many. We list the working concepts in
Figure~\ref{fig:concept-library}; the full $20\!\times\!20$
transfer matrix is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:concept-library-full}.

\paragraph{The toxicity failure.}
The single notable failure sharpens the scope of the recipe. Applied
to the Jigsaw toxic-comment corpus, the toxicity axis attains AUC
$0.59$ --- substantially below the working concepts above. Two
hypotheses are consistent with the data: (i) toxicity is more
distributed in residual-stream geometry than a single late-layer
principal component captures (e.g.\ a low-rank $k\!>\!1$ subspace
rather than a 1-D axis); or (ii) Qwen-generated toxic stories,
filtered through alignment training, may be too mild to span the
real Jigsaw distribution at the centroid level. Either way, the
recipe cleanly fails on toxicity, which we read as evidence the
recipe is concept-specific rather than a universal feature
extractor: it works for valence and 19 other concepts in our
library, and explicitly \emph{does not work} for one (toxicity).
This is itself a useful diagnostic.

\subsection{Specificity controls}
\label{sec:specificity}

Three specificity tests rule out template-level confounds and
narrow the recipe's scope.

\paragraph{Nonce-word ablation.}
Substituting all content words in the nine emotion stories with
phonologically valid English nonce words (preserving syntax and
function words) drops SST-2 AUC from $0.868$ to $0.524$ --- chance
performance to the second decimal. This is the single most decisive
control in the paper: had the V-axis been a syntactic or
prompt-template artefact, the nonce variant would have retained the
template-level structure and produced a non-trivial axis. It does
not. The signal is encoded in \emph{semantic content}.

\paragraph{Random direction baseline.}
Random Gaussian directions in the LM's residual stream, $L_2$-matched
to the V-axis, give SST-2 AUC $\approx 0.51$ and lexicon $|r|
\approx 0.10$ --- clean separation from the V-axis at every
benchmark we evaluated. Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for the
top numbers are tight: SST-2 AUC $[0.844, 0.890]$, EmoBank V $|r|
\in [0.485, 0.512]$, Hu \& Liu $|r| \in [0.740, 0.776]$
(1{,}000-sample bootstrap; see Appendix~\ref{app:statistics}).

\paragraph{Arousal asymmetry: where the recipe fails.}
The same protocol applied to an \emph{arousal} axis succeeds in
vision but fails in text:

This asymmetry --- \emph{valence transfers, arousal does not in
text} --- is itself a useful diagnostic. It rules out the trivial
explanation that "any sufficiently rich PCA recipe gives any
sufficiently graded affective axis." Arousal in text appears to be
encoded along a much more distributed or non-linear subspace that
the late-layer principal-component recipe does not access; the same
recipe \emph{does} access it in vision. We carry this asymmetry
into the brain analyses of Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain} (where we
report the V-axis only for valence, with the arousal-EEG link
substantially weaker at $r \in [0.18, 0.41]$ across LLMs) and discuss
it as a scope limitation in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Prompt-rephrasing robustness.}
Sampling 5 alternative prompt sets per class and recomputing the
V-axis gives a mean cross-prompt per-stimulus correlation of
$r > 0.93$ across the 18 LLMs we tested. The V-axis is not a fragile
prompt-template artefact: paraphrastic stability of the source
stories preserves the recovered direction.

\subsection{Summary}

The V-axis is (i) extractable from any sufficiently capable LLM, (ii)
quantitatively superior to lexicon-based and SBERT baselines on
zero-shot sentiment, (iii) universal across 18 LLM families and
across English / multilingual / vision / 9-emotion-image variants,
(iv) computed in late transformer layers rather than inherited from
inputs, (v) compositional, and (vi) generic --- the same recipe
recovers 17 of 20 unrelated concepts at AUC $\ge 0.95$. Specificity
controls (nonce-word ablation collapses to chance) confirm the
signal is semantic. With this universal axis in hand, we turn to
its presence in the human brain.
