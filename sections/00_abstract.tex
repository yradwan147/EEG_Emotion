\begin{abstract}
A central question at the interface of neuroscience and machine learning asks
whether the latent representations learned by language models bear meaningful
relation to those used by the human brain. We answer affirmatively for the
case of \emph{valence}, the principal axis of human emotion. From nine short
emotion stories --- one per category in the FACED 9-class taxonomy --- we
extract a single one-dimensional valence direction (the \textbf{V-axis}) that
generalizes universally: it is shared across 18 large language models from
0.5B to 72B parameters, recovers gold-standard sentiment lexicons (NRC,
Warriner) at $|r|\!\ge\!0.76$, and predicts the cohort-averaged EEG responses
of 123 human subjects watching emotional videos at $r=0.87$
($p<10^{-9}$). The brain encodes the V-axis most strongly in posterior
visual cortex --- challenging the classical frontal-asymmetry account for
video-evoked emotion --- with a significant contribution dominated by the
contrast between three emotional poles (anger, amusement, tenderness)
across nine stimuli, while the remaining nineteen stimuli contribute zero
signal.

We then show that EEG emotion classifiers trained on standard cross-entropy
\emph{spontaneously converge} to the same valence axis. Across 36
checkpoints spanning two architectures (CBraMod, EMOD), accuracy
correlates with V-axis encoding strength at $r\!=\!+0.885$ in the
class-mean subspace and $r\!=\!+0.715$ in the within-class residual
subspace; the per-checkpoint within-class residual $|r|$ predicts that
checkpoint's leave-one-out contribution to ensemble accuracy at
$r\!=\!+0.74$ ($p\!=\!0.014$). We test 25 representation-alignment
interventions designed to inject the V-axis as auxiliary supervision;
all either fail or harm accuracy above an empirically located
saturation threshold --- alignment training only helps when the base
model has not yet discovered the axis on its own. The V-axis is
\emph{the} latent these models converge to, regardless of whether we
ask them to.

We turn this principle into a new state-of-the-art on FACED 9-class
emotion classification: \textbf{0.6948 balanced accuracy
($+21.5\%$ relative over CBraMod, $+10.6\%$ over EmotionKD)} via uniform
softmax averaging across 10 checkpoints. The mechanism is principled: each
checkpoint shares the converged class-mean V-axis basin (saturated, free
from supervision), but encodes the within-class V-axis residual with
seed-specific noise; ensembling cancels this noise. A single val-selected
checkpoint also sets a new single-seed SOTA at \textbf{0.6755 balanced
accuracy}.

Our results unify three previously separate phenomena --- language-model
emotion latents, brain--LLM alignment, and EEG model ensembling --- under a
single principle: a universal valence axis is the natural latent of
emotion-classification systems built from text or scalp.
\end{abstract}
