\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
\vspace{-1mm}

We evaluate \ours across two benchmark datasets in four experimental settings: (1) within-subject evaluation on FACED and SEED-V, (2) cross-subject leave-group-out evaluation, (3) comprehensive ablation studies, and (4) analysis of LLM emotion geometry and error patterns. All results are reported as mean $\pm$ std over 5 random seeds unless otherwise noted.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}
\vspace{-1mm}

\noindent\textbf{FACED}~\cite{chen2023faced} contains 32-channel EEG recordings from 123 subjects watching 28 emotion-eliciting video clips spanning 9 emotion categories (amusement, inspiration, joy, tenderness, anger, fear, disgust, sadness, neutral). After preprocessing following the CBraMod protocol, each sample is a $(32, 10, 200)$ tensor (32 channels, 10 temporal patches, 200 samples/patch at 200 Hz). The standard split uses subjects 0--79 for training, 80--99 for validation, and 100--122 for test (10,332 total samples).

\noindent\textbf{SEED-V} contains 62-channel EEG recordings from 16 subjects across 3 sessions, with 5 emotion categories (happy, sad, fear, disgust, neutral). Each sample is a $(62, 1, 200)$ tensor---notably, only \emph{one} temporal patch, meaning only spatial attention is active in the criss-cross transformer. The dataset contains 115,001 samples ($\sim$12$\times$ more than FACED).

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\vspace{-1mm}

We use CBraMod~\cite{wang2025cbramod} (4.92M parameters, pretrained on TUEG) as the primary backbone, with cross-backbone validation on LaBraM~\cite{jiang2024labram} (\cref{sec:cross_backbone}). Training uses AdamW optimizer with multi-learning-rate scheduling (backbone: $10^{-4}$, heads: $5 \times 10^{-4}$), cosine annealing to $10^{-6}$, batch size 64, and 50 epochs. The semantic projection head is a 3-layer MLP with BatchNorm: $200 \to 512 \to 512 \to D_{\text{LLM}}$, inspired by SimCLR v2~\cite{chen2020simclrv2}. LLM emotion vectors are extracted from Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct ($D = 896$). KD hyperparameters: $\lambda = 0.1$, $\tau_t = 0.1$, $p_{\text{aug}} = 0.6$.

\cref{tab:aug_ablation} shows that temporal jitter is the single indispensable augmentation. Removing it drops performance by $-3.9\%$ (from $0.609$ to $0.569$), back to baseline level. All other augmentations can be removed with negligible ($\leq 0.4\%$) or even slightly positive effect. This finding has a biological interpretation: slight temporal shifts ($\pm 20$ samples at 200 Hz $= \pm 100$ ms) force the model to learn timing-invariant representations, critical for EEG where neural response onset varies across trials and subjects.

We sweep KD hyperparameters across 40 tasks (\cref{tab:kd_ablation}). Key findings: (1) $\lambda$ is robust in $[0.05, 0.10]$ but degrades sharply above $0.20$, indicating that the KD signal should complement rather than dominate the CE loss. (2) Sharper teacher temperature ($\tau_t = 0.05$) marginally helps. (3) Augmentation probability has the largest effect, with $p_{\text{aug}} = 0.6$ yielding the best result ($0.627$). Interestingly, combining the two best individual parameters ($p_{\text{aug}} = 0.6 + \tau_t = 0.05$) yields $0.619$---\emph{worse} than either alone---suggesting interference between sharper teacher distributions and heavier augmentation.

\cref{tab:baselines} presents a controlled comparison where all methods use the \emph{same} 3-layer BatchNorm projection head to isolate the effect of the loss function and teacher signal. Three findings emerge:

\noindent\textbf{(1) KD outperforms InfoNCE with identical architecture.} With the same projection head, LLM KD ($0.627$) beats InfoNCE ($0.614$) by $+1.3\%$, confirming that the improvement comes from the \emph{soft-label loss function}, not the projection head design.

\noindent\textbf{(2) LLM vectors outperform SBERT.} Replacing LLM hidden states with SBERT text embeddings~\cite{reimers2019sbert} (all-mpnet-base-v2, 768-dim) as KD targets yields $0.621$---better than InfoNCE but $0.6\%$ below LLM KD. This suggests that LLM hidden-state geometry captures emotion structure beyond surface-level text similarity.

\noindent\textbf{(3) Adapters help any contrastive loss.} Adapter64 + InfoNCE ($0.620$) outperforms vanilla InfoNCE ($0.614$), showing that adapters provide complementary task-specific adaptation independent of the loss function.

We systematically evaluate three PEFT approaches with frozen backbone: bottleneck adapters (rank 16/32/64/128), LoRA (rank 4/8/16), and FiLM conditioning (3/6 modulation layers).

\noindent\textbf{Adapter scaling} (\cref{fig:adapter_scaling}) shows a clear curve: $r=16$ (0.616) $\to$ $r=32$ (0.615) $\to$ $r=64$ (\textbf{0.628}) $\to$ $r=128$ (0.622). Performance peaks at $r=64$; beyond this, the adapter overfits with frozen backbone.

\noindent\textbf{LoRA comparison.} LoRA achieves $\sim$0.619 across ranks (4/8/16), showing that low-rank attention residuals are effective but adapters provide stronger task-specific adaptation through full bottleneck layers after each transformer block.

\noindent\textbf{FiLM conditioning} (0.612--0.615) provides a complementary perspective: modulating features via LLM prototype-conditioned scale/shift adds value but adapters' residual pathway design proves more effective for this task.

\noindent\textbf{Frozen backbone is optimal.} Training adapters with frozen backbone (0.628) outperforms unfreezing the backbone after adapter warmup (0.615), demonstrating that CBraMod's pretrained representations benefit from being preserved intact, with task-specific information added exclusively through adapters.

\begin{figure*}[!t]

    \caption{t-SNE visualization of learned EEG representations on the FACED test set. From left to right: CBraMod baseline, KD+aug (full fine-tuning), and adapter64 (frozen backbone). Emotion clusters become progressively more distinct with each component of \ours.}
    \label{fig:tsne}

\noindent\textbf{Error pattern.} EEG misclassification errors follow LLM semantic similarity (Spearman $r = 0.38$, $p = 3.4 \times 10^{-6}$). The effect is driven by negative emotion confusions ($64.6\%$ of within-category errors, $p = 3.2 \times 10^{-13}$). Controls show that SBERT text descriptions ($r = 0.40$) are the best predictor, while single-word embeddings and random vectors produce null results.

\noindent\textbf{Per-class improvement.} Confusion matrix analysis (\cref{fig:confusion}) shows the largest gains on high-arousal emotions: anger ($+26$ correct), fear ($+24$), inspiration ($+19$), while sadness---the only low-arousal negative emotion---shows a smaller gain ($+11$). This pattern is consistent with the LLM emotion geometry: the extracted vectors recover not just the valence axis but the full valence$\times$arousal circumplex, placing sadness apart from the high-arousal negative cluster (anger, fear, disgust). The KD loss exploits this two-dimensional structure, providing graded supervision that distinguishes both valence \emph{and} arousal.

\noindent\textbf{LLM universality.} Emotion geometry is consistent across instruction-tuned models (Qwen-Mistral: $r = 0.93$, Qwen-Phi2: $r = 0.52$), but absent in base models (Pythia: $r = 0.30$, Bloom: $r = 0.00$). Across 7 Qwen scales (0.5B--72B), valence correlation follows a U-shape (peak at 3B, dip at 32B, recovery at 72B), while cluster separation peaks at 1.5B and declines monotonically (see~\cref{sec:llm_scale}). For KD, the operationally relevant metric is cluster separation, making 1.5B optimal as a teacher.

\noindent\textbf{Per-subject analysis.} Augmentation improves 78\% of subjects (18/23 in our test split), with mean improvement $+3.8\%$ and maximum $+15.1\%$. Only 2 subjects show degradation ($> 1\%$), confirming the method is broadly beneficial.

\subsection{Cross-Backbone Generalization}
\label{sec:cross_backbone}
\vspace{-1mm}

A natural question is whether \ours is specific to the CBraMod backbone or generalizes across EEG foundation models. We evaluate on LaBraM~\cite{jiang2024labram} (ICLR 2024), which uses a different architecture (standard ViT-style transformer) and pretraining strategy (vector-quantized neural spectrum prediction on 2,500 hours).

\cref{tab:cross_backbone} shows a striking dataset-dependent pattern. On FACED, \ours produces significant improvements on both backbones: CBraMod ($+5.5\%$, $p < 10^{-5}$) and LaBraM ($+2.6\%$, $p = 0.025$), confirming that the KD benefit is architecture-agnostic. On SEED-V, KD has \emph{zero} measurable effect on either backbone---even testing three temperature settings ($\tau \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$) produced identical per-seed results for CBraMod. This dichotomy is explained by the token count: FACED provides 321 tokens per sample, sufficient for the KD gradient to influence learning, while SEED-V provides only 1 token (CBraMod) or 63 tokens (LaBraM), which is below the threshold where the KD signal can meaningfully contribute.

\paragraph{Token count requirement.}
An important practical finding is that KD requires sufficient tokens per EEG sample for the gradient signal to influence learning. On FACED (321 tokens per sample), KD produces significant improvements on both backbones. On SEED-V, CBraMod uses only 1 temporal patch (1 token) and LaBraM uses 63 tokens---in both cases, KD has zero effect. We tested three temperature settings ($\tau \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$) on SEED-V and obtained \emph{identical} per-seed results across all settings, confirming that the KD gradient is negligible when the representation is too compressed. This establishes a practical lower bound: $\gtrsim$300 tokens are needed for KD to meaningfully contribute.

\subsection{LLM Scale Analysis}
\label{sec:llm_scale}
\vspace{-1mm}

We extend the multi-LLM analysis (\cref{sec:supp_llm}) to investigate the effect of model scale on emotion vector quality. Evaluating Qwen2.5 models at 7 scales (0.5B to 72B parameters), we find a nuanced scale dependence: \emph{which metric is used determines which model is ``best.''} Valence correlation (Pearson $r$) peaks at 3B ($r = 0.976$), dips at 7--32B, then recovers at 72B ($r = 0.965$)---a U-shaped pattern. Cluster separation (inter-class vs.\ intra-class distance ratio) peaks at 1.5B ($2.04$) and declines monotonically with scale ($72\text{B}: 1.63$). The two metrics diverge because larger hidden dimensions ($896 \to 8192$) preserve the valence \emph{direction} (high $r$) while reducing inter-class discriminability (low separation)---the curse of dimensionality applied to emotion representations. For KD, cluster separation is the operationally relevant metric, as it determines the informativeness of soft labels. Thus 1.5B is optimal as a KD teacher, and practitioners should not assume that larger LLMs yield better teachers.

\subsection{SFT Negative Result}
\label{sec:sft_negative}
\vspace{-1mm}

We investigated whether emotion-specific supervised fine-tuning (SFT) of the teacher LLM improves vector quality. Using LoRA instruction tuning with 189 emotion classification/explanation tasks, cluster separation \emph{decreases} from $2.04$ (base) to $1.70$ ($-2.3\%$). Scaling to 1,898 instructions worsens the decline ($1.63$, $-6.3\%$), while the overall geometry remains stable (RDM agreement $r = 0.992$). The emotion geometry present in instruction-tuned models thus emerges from broad language modeling; further emotion-specific tuning tightens categorical boundaries without enriching inter-class structure, producing less informative soft labels. This parallels findings in brain--LLM alignment, where scaling---but not instruction tuning beyond a threshold---increases representational correspondence~\cite{scaling_brain_alignment2024}.
