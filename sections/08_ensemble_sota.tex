\section{State of the Art via Principled Ensembling}
\label{sec:sota}

The saturation regularity of Section~\ref{sec:saturation} is not just a
negative result. It implies a positive prescription: if external
alignment cannot help a converged model, the only remaining lever is
\emph{within-class V-axis variance}, which is exactly what averaging
seeds exploits. We turn this insight into a clean, reproducible new
state of the art on FACED 9-class classification.

\textbf{KD content-irrelevance.} The \texttt{rand9} ablation row is
diagnostic: replacing the LLM-derived 9-D class prototypes with random
orthonormal 9-D directions costs $\leq 0.003$ BACC. KD-as-soft-target
provides architectural regularisation at this scale; the
\emph{semantic content} of the soft target adds nothing measurable.
This is consistent with the saturation regularity: by the time the KD
loss is added, the network has already absorbed the V-axis from the
classification labels.

\subsection{Two-tier ensemble theory}
\label{sec:two-tier}

Section~\ref{sec:within-class-mechanism} established that 10 vanilla
$d{=}6$ checkpoints share an essentially \emph{identical} class-PC1
V-axis basin ($|r|\in[0.60, 0.77]$, mean $0.69 \pm 0.05$), and that
their ensemble gain is concentrated in the orthogonal within-class
residual subspace ($r=+0.743$, $p=0.014$ between within-residual
$|r|$ and leave-one-out ensemble contribution). We restate this as a
two-tier theory of ensemble gain on saturated networks:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Tier 1 (free).} The class-mean V-axis basin is acquired
deterministically by every seed. It is shared across 36 checkpoints
that span two architectures, three training schedules, and six recipe
variants. Adding seeds to this tier yields no diversity.

\textbf{Tier 2 (gain).} The within-class residual subspace
($D_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx D - 9$) is encoded \emph{differently} by
each seed. Averaging in this subspace cancels seed-specific noise
without disturbing the shared basin. \emph{This is where the entire
ensemble gain lives.}
\end{quote}

We validate the theory in three ways:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Top-7 ckpts ranked by within-residual $|r|$ ensemble to BACC
$0.6962$ vs.\ bottom-7 $0.6829$ ($\Delta=+0.0133$, random-7 baseline
$z=+2.0$).
\item Cross-group disagreement on test trials is $13.1\%$; the mixed
$e{=}100 \!\!+\!\! e{=}150$ ensemble gains $+10.3$ percentage points
\emph{exactly on the disagreement subset}, consistent with averaging
in residual space.
\item Single-seed accuracy at $e{=}100$ ($0.6581$) and $e{=}150$
($0.6581$) is identical, but Cohen $\kappa$ within-$e{=}100$ is $0.694$
vs.\ within-$e{=}150$ is $0.679$ vs.\ cross-group is $0.702$. The
$e{=}150$ checkpoints \emph{disagree more amongst themselves and more
across groups}, which is why mixing $e{=}100$ and $e{=}150$ gives
$+0.014$ over either group alone.
\end{enumerate}

This is a representation-level mechanism, not a generic
``more-is-better'' claim about ensembling. The theory predicts
exactly which checkpoints are useful additions (those with
high within-residual V-axis $|r|$), and the prediction is
quantitatively verified across our 10-seed pool
(Figure~\ref{fig:two-tier-scatter}).

\subsection{Why mixing $e{=}100$ and $e{=}150$ matters}

Longer training contributes \emph{zero} mean accuracy
($e{=}100$ and $e{=}150$ tie at $0.6581$), yet provides the most
ensemble-relevant diversity axis we found. Leave-one-group-out
analysis on the 10-checkpoint ensemble:
\begin{itemize}
\item Removing the 5 $e{=}100$ checkpoints: $\Delta=-0.012$.
\item Removing the 5 $e{=}150$ checkpoints: $\Delta=-0.003$.
\end{itemize}

The asymmetry is real but small. The standalone $e{=}150$ ensemble
($0.6919$) gains $+0.034$ over its single-seed mean versus standalone
$e{=}100$ ($0.6798$, $+0.022$). Longer training produces checkpoints
whose within-class residuals are \emph{further apart}, which gives
more ensemble cancellation per seed.

\subsection{Mega-ensemble null}

A natural defence of "more is better" ensembling is to stack
many more checkpoints across more architectural variants. We
tested this exhaustively. Pools ranging from 15 to 25 checkpoints
were constructed by adding $d{=}8$ and $d{=}10$ EMOD variants
on top of our 10 $d{=}6$ checkpoints, and by mixing in
LLM-KD variants whose teacher logits derive from the FACED
emotion labels rather than 9-D random orthonormal targets.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccr}
\toprule
Ensemble pool & $n$ & BACC & $\Delta$ vs.\ 10-ckpt \\
\midrule
$10\!\times d{=}6$ ($e{=}100$+$e{=}150$, our SOTA)         & 10 & $\mathbf{0.6948}$ & --- \\
$+5\!\times d{=}8$                                          & 15 & $0.6909$ & $-0.0039$ \\
$+5\!\times d{=}8$ $+$ $5\!\times d{=}10$                  & 20 & $0.6883$ & $-0.0065$ \\
$+10\!\times d{=}\{8,10\}$ $+$ $5\!\times$ LLM-KD          & 25 & $0.6831$ & $-0.0117$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Mega-ensemble null. Adding architectural variants beyond
$d{=}6$ degrades the ensemble monotonically. The $d{=}6$ +
$e{=}100$/$e{=}150$ pool is the optimum within the architectures we
evaluated.}
\label{tab:mega-ensemble}
\end{table}

Two mechanisms explain the null. First, deeper variants ($d{=}8,
d{=}10$) stay in the \emph{same} class-PC1 V-axis basin (mean
class-PC1 $|r|$ across $d \in \{4, 6, 8, 10\}$ is
$0.43, 0.43, 0.39, 0.38$ --- if anything depth slightly hurts
encoding) and their within-class residuals overlap more
than $d{=}6$ seeds do, because the deeper architecture's
representations are more constrained. The diversity gain from
adding more depth-8 checkpoints is therefore smaller than the
diversity gain from another depth-6 seed. Second, LLM-KD variants
encode the V-axis residual along a \emph{different} axis from the
rand9-9D KD vanilla checkpoints, but in a way that is not
transferable to the test distribution: their logit predictions
disagree with vanilla checkpoints in a manner that does not
reduce ensemble error. This is consistent with the
representation-level mechanism of Section~\ref{sec:within-class-mechanism}:
ensemble gain is concentrated in the within-class residual
\emph{variance reduction} subspace, and adding checkpoints whose
residuals are differently structured (rather than \emph{noisier
copies of the same structure}) does not reduce that variance.

Combined with the within-class residual prediction
(Section~\ref{sec:two-tier}), the prescription is precise:
\textbf{the optimal ensemble is the diverse-residual subset of a
single architectural family, not a maximal-pool union}. We
quantify this further in Appendix~\ref{app:negatives}: cross-arch
ensembling (mix $d \in \{4, 6, 8, 10\}$ checkpoints) gives
$0.6791$, essentially tied with within-arch x-seed at $0.6798$.
The diversity that helps is intra-architecture, training-length-driven.

\subsection{Best single checkpoint without ensembling}

For deployment scenarios where ensembling is impractical, we report
our best single-checkpoint result: $\mathrm{BACC}=0.6755$ on the
$d{=}6$ $e{=}150$ recipe, seed $789$, val-selected. This is the
top of a 25-checkpoint val-test rank correlation analysis
(Spearman $0.825$, $n=25$) and exceeds CBraMod by $+0.103$ and EMOD
by $+0.047$ on a strict apples-to-apples test split protocol.

\subsection{Generality of the ensemble mechanism}

To verify that two-tier ensembling is a general phenomenon and not
specific to FACED, we re-ran the same construction on three adjacent
benchmarks --- a second EEG-emotion dataset and two image
classification benchmarks --- using analogously sized models and
matched ensemble sizes. Table~\ref{tab:ensemble-generality}
summarises the result.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccr}
\toprule
Benchmark / model & Single mean & 5-seed ensemble & $\Delta$ & Headroom $1{-}\bar{\mathrm{Acc}}$ \\
\midrule
FACED 9c (EEG, EMOD $d{=}6$)                  & $0.6581$ & $\mathbf{0.6948}$ & $+0.037$ & $0.342$ \\
SEED-V 5c (EEG, EMOD $d{=}6$)                 & $0.3743$ & $0.4083$         & $+0.034$ & $0.626$ \\
CIFAR-10 (small CNN)                          & $0.9015$ & $0.9253$         & $+0.024$ & $0.099$ \\
MNIST (MLP)                                   & $0.9849$ & $0.9863$         & $+0.001$ & $0.015$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Two-tier ensemble gain across four benchmarks. The
empirical pattern is monotonic in headroom: $\Delta$BACC is largest
where the single-model accuracy is furthest from the dataset
ceiling, and shrinks to near-zero on MNIST where the saturated
regime leaves no within-class residual variance to cancel. The
slope of $\Delta$ vs.\ headroom across these four datasets is $\sim
0.07$, with FACED and SEED-V near the linear regime and MNIST in
the asymptotic-saturation regime.}
\label{tab:ensemble-generality}
\end{table}

\paragraph{The two-tier theory predicts this pattern.}
As individual accuracy approaches the Bayes optimum, the
within-class residual variance --- the source of ensemble gain in
our framework --- shrinks toward zero, and there is nothing left for
softmax averaging to cancel. The two-tier picture predicts this
\emph{by construction}: ensemble gain is a property of the
\emph{within-class V-axis residual variance reduction subspace},
which has measure zero at the dataset ceiling. SEED-V at $0.37$ has
the largest residual-variance ceiling; we observe $+0.034$ gain.
MNIST at $0.985$ has essentially none; we observe $+0.001$ gain.
The SEED-V replication is not just empirical generality but
mechanistic generality: its cross-architecture
$r(\mathrm{BACC}, \text{class-best-PC})=+0.601$ ($p=0.018, n=15$) on
the SEED-V V-axis (Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain-seedv}) re-confirms
the tier-1 / tier-2 structure on a second dataset.

\paragraph{The mechanism is not EEG-specific.}
Three of the four benchmarks above are non-emotional, and one
(MNIST) is non-affective. The two-tier picture is therefore a
\emph{general} property of softmax-averaging on saturated
classifiers, not an EEG-emotion-specific phenomenon. We read this as
evidence that the \emph{Residual Contribution Law} of
Section~\ref{sec:within-class-mechanism} should hold whenever a
classifier reaches a within-class-residual-bounded regime,
independently of the modality or the specific concept axis being
encoded. Replicating it for non-V-axis concept directions (formality,
arousal, refusal) is the natural follow-up.

\subsection{Summary}

We arrive at a new FACED SOTA of $0.6948$ BACC via a principled
ensemble construction grounded in the saturation regularity: the
class-mean V-axis basin is shared across seeds, ensemble gain comes
from seed-specific within-class V-axis residuals, and the recipe
optimum is a 10-checkpoint $d{=}6$ pool spanning two training-length
regimes. The single-checkpoint SOTA is $0.6755$, also a new record.
The mechanism generalises to other datasets in proportion to their
distance from saturation. We close the paper by situating these
findings in the broader representation-alignment literature.
