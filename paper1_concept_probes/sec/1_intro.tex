Large language models (LLMs) encode rich affective representations, yet the
exact form of these representations is poorly understood. A natural question
is whether sentiment, emotion, or affect is encoded as a \emph{linear direction}
in some hidden layer of the LLM's residual stream, and if so, how we can extract
this direction with minimal supervision. Traditional linear probing approaches
\cite{alain2016understanding,hewitt2019structural} require thousands of labeled
examples to fit a probe on a chosen layer; activation-steering approaches
\cite{rimsky2024steering,turner2024activation} require similarly large sets of
paired contrastive examples. In this work we show that a much simpler and more
data-efficient approach is possible.

\paragraph{Our method in one sentence.}
Generate nine short stories for each of nine emotion categories
(\texttt{amusement, inspiration, joy, tenderness, anger, fear, disgust, sadness,
neutral}) using the instruction-tuned LLM itself; extract the mean-pooled
final-layer hidden state of each story; average per class into nine centroids;
take the first principal component of the mean-centered centroid matrix as the
valence axis.

That single 1\,536-dimensional direction, computed in seconds from 81 fully
LLM-generated and completely unlabeled samples, turns out to be a
\emph{competitive zero-shot sentiment classifier} across a wide range of
downstream tasks. It produces AUC \sstauc{} on SST-2 movie reviews, AUC
\imdbauc{} on IMDB long-form reviews, AUC \yelpauc{} on Yelp Polarity
restaurant reviews, AUC \twauc{} on SemEval-2017 Twitter sentiment, AUC
\mrauc{} on Rotten Tomatoes, and correlates with human-annotated valence lexicons
at Pearson $|r|=\huliur{}$ (Hu \& Liu \cite{huliu2004opinion}, 6{,}789 words)
and \warrinerr{} (Warriner \textit{et al.\@} \cite{warriner2013norms}, sampled
to 3{,}000 words). All of these results use the \emph{same} nine story
centroids.

\paragraph{A striking asymmetry.}
The same method, applied to the second PCA component, does \emph{not}
recover a usable arousal axis: on EmoBank
\cite{buechel2017emobank} continuous arousal ratings the zero-shot Pearson
correlation is $|r|\leq 0.16$, essentially at the random-projection floor. This
asymmetry is remarkably consistent: across three causal model families (Qwen,
GPT-NeoX, BLOOM), five model scales (0.5B to 7B parameters), five languages
(English, Spanish, French, German, Chinese), and seven downstream sentiment
tasks, the valence direction is linear and extractable while the arousal
direction is not. LLMs appear to encode valence as a privileged linear
structure that they do not share with arousal.

\paragraph{Where in the network does this direction live?}
A fine-grained layer sweep of Qwen2.5-1.5B reveals a novel ``V-shape''
interpretability pattern. The valence direction is \emph{strong} at layer 1
($\mathrm{AUC}\!=\!0.75$), then \emph{dies} through the middle layers
($\mathrm{AUC}\!\leq\!0.53$ for layers 2--26), then \emph{suddenly re-crystallizes}
at layer 27 ($\mathrm{AUC}\!=\!0.84$) and remains there at layer 28. Further
analysis using cross-layer V-axis cosine similarities shows that the V-axis at
layer 14 is \emph{nearly orthogonal} (cosine $0.07$) to the V-axis at layer 28,
while the V-axis at layer 27 is 95\% aligned with layer 28. The final two
transformer blocks compute a valence direction that is not inherited from
earlier layers; it is freshly constructed just before the next-token
distribution is formed.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item We introduce \ours, a data-free few-shot concept probing method that
requires nine LLM-generated examples per class, no labels, and no gradient
updates. The method produces a linear concept direction in
$<\!1$~minute on a single V100.
\item We show that this direction is competitive with supervised classifiers
on seven sentiment benchmarks, two word-valence lexicons, and one continuous
affect regression task. On SST-2 we match the accuracy of a supervised
logistic regression trained on 80--100 labeled examples while using
\emph{zero} labels.
\item We demonstrate data efficiency in the extreme: a single story per class
(9 total samples) already produces an SST-2 AUC of 0.74, and just nine stories
per class match a supervised logistic regression trained on roughly 80--100
labeled SST-2 examples --- a 10--30$\times$ reduction in labeled data.
\item We reveal a novel interpretability pattern: a sudden re-crystallization
of the valence direction at the final 1--2 transformer blocks, separated from
an embedding-level signal at layer~1 by a long middle-layer ``dilution zone''.
This V-shape holds across Qwen, GPT-NeoX, and BLOOM model families.
\item We report a sharp valence/arousal asymmetry: the same method extracts
valence with Pearson $|r|=\emobankr{}$ on EmoBank but fails to extract arousal
at any layer in any model (mean $|r|\!\leq\!0.16$).
\item We show cross-language partial sharing: V-axes extracted from stories in
English, Spanish, French, and German have pairwise cosine similarities in the
$0.23$--$0.47$ range, well above random, while the corresponding arousal axes
are essentially orthogonal.
\item Probing is \speedup{} faster than direct-prompting baselines at
inference time, produces continuous scores useful for ranking and calibration,
and is data-free (no prompt engineering required).
\end{itemize}

We also include a set of honest negative findings that shape the paper's
scope: direct prompting outperforms probing on SST-2 by 15 percentage points
(92.78\% vs.\ 77.75\%), which we view as evidence that probing's value is
\emph{not} raw classification SOTA but rather interpretability, efficiency,
and data-free applicability; applying the same method to concept generalization
(toxicity) does \emph{not} transfer to the Jigsaw Civil Comments benchmark
(AUC $0.59$), indicating that the method is valence-specialized rather than
fully general; and cross-LLM ensembling of V-axes \emph{hurts} (AUC drops by
$0.02$--$0.04$ below the best single LLM) because lower-capacity models drag
down the ensemble. These findings define \emph{when} \ours works and when it
does not.
