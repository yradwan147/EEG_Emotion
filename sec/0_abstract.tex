\begin{abstract}
\label{sec:abstract-sec}
Recent interpretability research reveals that large language models develop internal emotion representations organized along the same valence-arousal dimensions found in human psychology.
We introduce \textbf{\ours}, which exploits this brain-LLM alignment: we extract emotion vectors from instruction-tuned LLMs and use their relational geometry as soft-label knowledge distillation targets for EEG emotion classification, teaching the model that \emph{anger is closer to disgust than to joy} rather than treating all misclassifications equally.
On FACED (9-class, 123 subjects), \ours achieves \(\mathbf{0.628}\) balanced accuracy, improving over the previous state of the art (REVE, NeurIPS 2025) by \(\mathbf{+11.2\%}\) relative, with cross-subject generalization confirmed at $p < 0.001$.
On SEED-V (5-class, 62 channels), Cohen's $\kappa = \mathbf{0.275}$ improves over CBraMod (ICLR 2025) by \(\mathbf{+7.0\%}\).
Through systematic comparison against supervised contrastive learning and valence-arousal baselines (\numconfigs configurations, \numruns runs), we show that only the LLM emotion structure---not generic contrastive signals---provides improvement beyond augmentation alone.
A key finding is that freezing the pretrained backbone and adding lightweight adapters (310K parameters) matches full fine-tuning (4.9M parameters): the critical factor is the \emph{quality of supervision}, not model capacity.
We further demonstrate that LLM emotion geometry is universal across instruction-tuned models from 6 families but absent in base models, establishing instruction tuning as the mechanism through which this structure emerges.
We release our code and emotion vectors to advance brain-language alignment research.
\end{abstract}
