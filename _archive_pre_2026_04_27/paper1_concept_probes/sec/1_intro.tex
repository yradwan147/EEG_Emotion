Practical affect classification still runs on labels: thousands of
annotations to train a sentiment model, hand-curated multilingual
datasets to support non-English deployment, bespoke contrastive pairs to
probe LLM internals. Each of those requirements is a barrier in the
real-world settings where sentiment analysis is most needed --- new
languages, new domains (legal, medical, financial), and rapid-prototyping
pipelines where no labels have yet been collected. A natural question is
whether the \emph{content} those labels are trying to recover is already
present --- in linear form --- inside any sufficiently capable
instruction-tuned LLM, and if so whether we can pull it out without
labels.

This paper answers the question affirmatively for valence. A single
unsupervised direction, computed in seconds from 81 LLM-generated
paragraphs and zero human annotations, is a competitive zero-shot
sentiment classifier across five English benchmarks, four non-English
languages, three word-valence lexicons, one continuous VAD regression,
and two concept-generalisation probes --- seventeen evaluations
in total, all using the \emph{same} 81-story axis. The same axis
classifies held-out Chinese stories at AUC $1.000$ without ever
seeing Chinese during extraction.

\paragraph{Our method in one sentence.}
Generate nine short stories for each of nine emotion categories
(\texttt{amusement, inspiration, joy, tenderness, anger, fear, disgust,
sadness, neutral}) using the instruction-tuned LLM itself; extract the
mean-pooled final-layer hidden state of each story; average per class into
nine centroids; take the first principal component of the mean-centered
centroid matrix as the valence axis.

The direction has four properties that together make it a useful
deployment-oriented probe:

\paragraph{1. Unsupervised and data-efficient.}
The axis is extracted with zero labels, zero gradient updates, and zero
human-written contrastive pairs. On SST-2, it matches the accuracy of a
supervised logistic regression trained on $N\!\approx\!100$ labelled
examples on the same hidden features, a $\sim\!10$--$30\times$
data-efficiency gain. A single story per class (9 total samples) already
gives AUC $0.74$ on SST-2; nine stories per class reaches $0.868$.

\paragraph{2. Cross-language transfer without translation.}
The \emph{English} V-axis, applied zero-shot to held-out non-English
stories, recovers valence at AUC $0.949$ (Spanish), $0.991$ (French),
$0.972$ (German), and \emph{$1.000$} (Chinese) --- no translation, no
re-extraction, no target-language labels. The direction the LLM places
valence on is shared across the languages it was trained on.

\paragraph{3. Robust across model scales and families.}
The identical recipe produces a usable V-axis on Qwen2.5-0.5B, 1.5B, and
7B (all $|r|\!=\!0.48$--$0.49$ on EmoBank V), Pythia-1.4b ($|r|\!=\!0.46$),
and Bloom-560m ($|r|\!=\!0.34$). Across four LLMs the per-story valence
rankings agree at mean cosine $0.961$ and Spearman $0.954$. The method is
not tied to a particular pretraining recipe.

\paragraph{4. Beats the standard contrastive recipe.}
Representation-engineering methods extract concept directions from
operator-specified contrastive pairs (honest/dishonest, happy/sad,
harmful/harmless)
\cite{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering, arditi2024refusal}. On
matched Qwen-1.5B L28 features, our 9-centroid PCA improves SST-2 AUC
by $+0.115$ ($0.868$ vs $0.753$) over the mean-difference contrastive
axis computed from the \emph{same} stories repartitioned into
positive/negative halves, while removing the requirement that the
operator supply a binary split at all.

\paragraph{A scope statement, not a classifier race.}
\ours{} is not competing for state-of-the-art sentiment accuracy. Direct
few-shot prompting of the same LLM reaches $92.78\%$ on SST-2 ($+15$
percentage points over our median-threshold probe); a full-rank
supervised logistic regression on the \emph{same} hidden features
reaches AUC $0.911$ ($+0.043$). What we offer is the most data-efficient
one-dimensional \emph{summary} of valence extractable under a strict
zero-label constraint, together with evidence that the same summary
transfers across languages, tasks, and model families. A
counterfactual ablation (\cref{sec:counterfactual}) makes the scope
honest: projecting our axis out of the residual stream changes full-rank
LogReg by only $0.001$ AUC. Valence is distributed across many
directions; our axis is the most useful single one we can extract
without labels.

\paragraph{Where does this direction live?}
A fine-grained layer sweep of Qwen2.5-1.5B reveals a novel ``V-shape''
pattern. The valence direction is \emph{strong} at layer 1
($\mathrm{AUC}\!=\!0.75$, inherited from token embeddings), \emph{fades}
through the middle ($\mathrm{AUC}\!\leq\!0.53$ for layers 2--26), and
\emph{re-crystallizes abruptly} at layer 27 ($\mathrm{AUC}\!=\!0.84$)
and 28. The L14 and L28 V-axes are nearly orthogonal (cosine $0.07$);
L27 and L28 are $95\%$ aligned. The final-layer V-axis is not inherited
from the middle of the network --- it is freshly constructed just before
the LM head. The pattern replicates across three causal families (Qwen,
GPT-NeoX, BLOOM) and \emph{vanishes} in bidirectional encoders (BERT,
RoBERTa), whose V-axis peaks in the middle. Combined with the
cross-language result, the picture is consistent: valence is a
universal linear direction in causal LMs, built at the top of the
network where the LM head reads it.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item We introduce \ours, a fully unsupervised concept-probing recipe
that produces a usable linear valence direction from nine LLM-generated
stories per class --- no labels, no gradient updates, no contrastive
pairs --- in under one minute on a single V100.
\item We demonstrate that the extracted valence axis transfers to
\emph{nine} languages (EN/ES/FR/DE/ZH plus pairwise cross-lingual
cosines), five LLM families, and seventeen zero-label evaluations with
the same 81-story axis, including SST-2 (AUC $0.868$), IMDB ($0.895$),
Yelp ($0.953$), Twitter SemEval-17 ($0.923$), Rotten Tomatoes ($0.843$),
Hu \& Liu lexicon ($|r|=0.76$), Warriner 3k lexicon ($|r|=0.69$), and
EmoBank continuous valence ($|r|=0.50$).
\item We report a principal-angle analysis showing that PC1 of the nine
centroids captures $55.8\%$ of their variance (vs.\ the next singular value
at $15.2\%$, a $1.9\times$ separation) and lies $89.9^\circ$ from PC2 by
construction; random directions in the same $1\,536$-dim space have mean
angle $88.8^\circ \pm 0.8^\circ$ from PC1, confirming that PC1 is not a
trivial variance-axis artifact.
\item We quantify the method's positioning with a counterfactual ablation:
removing our axis from the residual stream changes supervised full-rank
logistic regression by only $0.001$ AUC, showing that valence is
distributed across many directions. Our contribution is therefore not
``the valence direction'' but the most useful data-efficient
1-D summary of valence extractable under a zero-label constraint.
\item We compare directly to the contrastive mean-difference extraction
of \cite{zou2023representation, rimsky2024steering, arditi2024refusal}: on
the same Qwen-1.5B L28 features, contrastive-pair mean-diff gives SST-2
AUC $0.753$; PCA-of-nine-centroids gives $0.868$ ($+0.115$).
\item We reveal a ``V-shape'' layer-wise emergence pattern: the valence
direction is present at L1, absent through the middle, and re-crystallizes
at the final 1--2 blocks. This pattern replicates across Qwen, Pythia, and
Bloom, and does not appear in bidirectional encoders.
\item We include a scope statement: arousal is not recovered by the same
PCA-of-centroids recipe ($|r|\leq 0.16$ at every layer in every model),
delineating the regime of applicability to valence-like global concepts
aligned with the linear representation hypothesis
\cite{park2024linear}.
\end{itemize}
