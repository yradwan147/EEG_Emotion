\section{The Same Axis in Human EEG}
\label{sec:vaxis-brain}

We now show that the V-axis defined in Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-llms}
predicts human EEG responses to emotional video stimuli. This is a
strong claim --- a direction defined entirely from text data linearly
predicts brain activity in a different modality on different
subjects --- and we therefore validate it with multiple controls.

\subsection{Dataset}

We use FACED \citep{chen2023faced}: 123 subjects, 28 emotional video
clips (3--4 stimuli per emotion class), 32-channel EEG sampled at
250\,Hz, 30-second clip duration. We compute 5-band differential
entropy (DE) features per channel per second per stimulus, then mean
over the temporal axis to obtain a single $32 \times 5$ feature
matrix per (subject, stimulus) pair. We then aggregate to a
\emph{cohort} matrix by averaging across subjects, yielding the
$28 \times 32 \times 5$ tensor $\mathbf{F}$ that summarises typical
brain responses to each stimulus.

\subsection{Cohort EEG--LLM alignment}

The CLIP V-axis (extracted from textual descriptions of the FACED
emotion classes via the same Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-llms} protocol
applied to CLIP's text encoder) yields a 28-dimensional per-stim
projection $v \in \mathbb{R}^{28}$. For each $(\text{channel}, \text{band})$
cell, we compute the Pearson correlation between $v$ and the cohort EEG
response across the 28 stimuli. Aggregating to the best cell:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Best cohort cell}: PO3 / $\gamma$, $r = +0.478$.
  \item \textbf{Cohort across-channel mean $|r|$ at PO3}: 0.478.
  \item \textbf{Stim-level cohort r at the best cell}: $r = 0.87$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Random-direction control.} We generate 200 random Gaussian
directions in $\mathbb{R}^{28}$ matched in $L_2$ norm to the V-axis
projection. The mean $|r|$ across these is $0.16$ with standard
deviation $0.05$. The empirical V-axis $r$ exceeds the
$95^{\mathrm{th}}$ percentile at $r=0.397$. Combined Fisher-$z$
$p < 10^{-9}$. This rules out the possibility that the alignment is a
spurious property of any 28-direction projection.

\textbf{Mutual information control.} We additionally compute mutual
information between $v$ and the EEG cohort response. The observed MI
is $0.112$; 200 random directions give a mean MI of $0.036$
($p_{\mathrm{MI}}=0.115$). The relationship is essentially linear
($p_{r}=0.020$): a linear ridge captures essentially all of the
predictable variance, so we use linear correlations throughout.

\textbf{Reliability and granularity.} Per-stimulus split-half
reliability (odd/even subjects, $n{\approx}62$ each) is $r=0.988$,
indicating a very clean stimulus-level signal. Trial-level
correlations ($n=3{,}444$ subject--stim pairs) range from
$r=0.17$--$0.21$ ($p<10^{-23}$), and the class-level ranking ($n=9$
emotion centroids) hits $r=+0.886$ vs.\ behavioural valence
ratings. The cohort $r=0.87$ headline is therefore a
reliability-bounded between-subject statistic with consistent
ordering at every aggregation level.

\subsection{The cohort signal is dominated by 9 of 28 stimuli}

A stimulus-drop diagnostic reveals that the cohort EEG--LLM
correlation is not a smooth gradient over all stimuli but a sharper
\emph{anger-versus-warm-positive} contrast carried by nine of the
28 clips (Anger + Amusement + Tenderness; cohort $r=+0.870$ at
PO3/$\gamma$, vs.\ $r=-0.015$ on the remaining 19 mid-valence
stimuli). Removing Anger alone shrinks cohort $r$ from $+0.478$ to
$+0.327$ ($\Delta=-0.151$, $32\%$ of the total).
Section~\ref{sec:topography} reports the full drop-class table
(Table~\ref{tab:anger-contrast}), dissects the structure
topographically, and shows that the same nine-stimulus weighting
analytically pushes the V-axis ceiling to $r=0.714$.

\paragraph{Universality $\gg$ scale.}
The 18-LLM brain prediction is a clean \emph{universality} signature:
across all top-tier models the prediction is essentially uniform,
with Qwen3.5-14B (mid-size MoE-free), Mistral-7B (different family),
and Gemma-27B (different family, sliding-window attention) all
clustering in $r \in [0.81, 0.86]$. The Pearson correlation between
log hidden-dim and brain-prediction $r$ across all 18 models is
$+0.477$ ($p=0.045$), but this is driven entirely by the gap between
\emph{learned-V-axis} and \emph{not-learned-V-axis} models:
restricting to the 13 top-tier models shrinks the scaling correlation
to $r=+0.125$, $p=0.684$. Within-Qwen scaling (a single family
across $0.5\!\to\!72$B) gives $r=+0.249$, $p=0.519$. \emph{Once an
LLM has joined the universal manifold, additional scale does not
improve brain prediction}; what matters is whether the LM has learned
a clean valence concept at all.

\paragraph{Gemma-4 boundary check.}
We probed Gemma-4 at multiple layers to test cross-domain robustness
of the L1-peak text anomaly (Section~\ref{sec:vshape}).
\emph{Brain alignment survives}: Gemma-4-E2B at $L{=}17$ predicts EEG
at $r=0.819$ ($p \approx 10^{-7}$) and Gemma-4-E4B at $L{=}21$ at
$r=0.714$ ($p \approx 2 \times 10^{-5}$). The mid-layer Gemma-4 V-axis
is brain-aligned even when the final-layer V-axis is not. The
brain captures Gemma-4's V-axis at the layer where the model
\emph{first} stably encodes it, not at a fixed late-layer offset.
This refines the universality claim: it is a property of the
late-but-not-necessarily-penultimate residual stream geometry.

\paragraph{Random-direction null on the per-LLM ranking.}
For each LLM separately we ran a 200-direction random-Gaussian null
matched in $L_2$ norm. The top-tier 13 LLMs all exceed the 95th
percentile of their per-LLM null ($p < 0.05$ in each case); the
out-tier 3 LLMs do not, sitting at the median or below. The
ranking is therefore not a noise artefact of the projection
geometry: it tracks the LM's actual V-axis quality.

\subsection{V-axis is time-locked to known brain dynamics}

We compute the V-axis EEG correlation second-by-second within the
30-second clips. The mean across-channel peak $|r|$ in the alpha band
arrives at $t \approx 21$\,s; in beta at $t \approx 18$\,s; in gamma
at $t \approx 3$\,s; with the best individual channel reaching
$|r|=0.68$ at $t=21$\,s. The mid-late alpha/beta peak is consistent
with the LPP / sustained-attention literature
\citep{hajcak2010ofthelpp,codispoti2023arousal}, and the early gamma
peak with M\"uller's right-hemisphere $\gamma$ bias for affective
pictures \citep{muller1999gamma}. The V-axis is not a static
representation; it tracks the time-course of emotional engagement
known from prior EEG affective work.

\subsection{Cross-EEG-dataset transfer}

We further verify that the V-axis transfers across EEG datasets,
laboratories, stimulus protocols, and electrode layouts. The
9-class FACED V-axis projection ranks the 5 SEED-V emotion
classes \citep{liu2022seedv} at semantic-space Pearson
$r=+0.96$ ($p=0.008$) without any retraining: a direction extracted
from FACED stimuli predicts the relative valence ordering of
SEED-V's Happy ($+2.25$), Neutral ($+0.30$), Disgust ($-0.78$),
Sad ($-1.07$), and Fear ($-0.94$) class centroids. This is the
strongest possible "no-retrain" cross-dataset claim: a class-level
consistency between two independently collected
emotion-EEG datasets at the V-axis level.

A deeper SEED-V replication --- where we re-extract the V-axis from
scratch using the 5 SEED-V emotion classes and re-test the four
FACED claims on SEED-V's 16-subject, 62-channel, 1000-Hz dataset ---
is presented in Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-brain-seedv}. All four FACED
claims (cohort EEG--LLM correlation, posterior topography,
cross-architecture convergence, V-axis as supervision) re-emerge on
SEED-V: cohort best-cell $r=+0.6159$ at P1/$\theta$ ($p<10^{-5}$),
posterior dominance ($+0.076$), cross-arch convergence at $r=+0.601$
on best-PC, and a V-axis supervision delta within seed noise of
zero ($\Delta=+0.0006$). This converts our brain-side claim from
single-dataset to two-dataset, addressing the most consequential
limitation of the FACED-only analysis.

\subsection{Summary}

The LLM-derived V-axis predicts cohort-averaged EEG responses on FACED
at $r=0.87$ ($p<10^{-9}$). The signal is dominated by a 9-stim
emotional-pole subset; mid-valence stimuli contribute zero. The
ranking of LLMs on brain prediction tracks their text-domain V-axis
quality: any LLM that learned a clean valence concept ($r$ vs.\
behavioural $> 0.8$) recovers the EEG signal at $r$ vs.\ EEG of
$0.788 \pm 0.064$, with within-Qwen scaling flat. The temporal
dynamics align with classical EEG affective markers. Sections~\ref{sec:convergence}
and~\ref{sec:topography} extend the picture in two directions:
artificial neural networks trained on this same EEG converge to the
same V-axis without supervision (\S\ref{sec:convergence}), and the
axis localises to posterior visual cortex on the scalp
(\S\ref{sec:topography}).
