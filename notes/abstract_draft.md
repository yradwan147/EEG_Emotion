# Abstract — Drafts

## V1 — punchy, ML-leaning

We discover that EEG emotion-classification models trained on standard cross-entropy supervision spontaneously converge to a one-dimensional valence direction extracted from large language models, without any explicit alignment objective. The same direction predicts the EEG responses of 123 human subjects watching emotional videos at cohort-level Pearson correlation 0.87 (p < 10⁻⁹), and is most strongly encoded in posterior visual cortex rather than the frontal regions implicated by classical asymmetry hypotheses. Across 36 checkpoints spanning two architectures and three training regimes, the strength with which a model encodes this universal valence axis predicts its accuracy at Pearson r = +0.74 in the within-class residual subspace (p = 0.014). We test 25 representation-alignment interventions designed to inject this axis as auxiliary supervision; all fail or hurt above a saturation threshold that we locate empirically — alignment training only helps when the base model has not yet found the axis on its own. The valence axis is *the* latent these models converge to, regardless of whether we ask them to. We turn this finding into a state-of-the-art EEG emotion classifier (0.6948 balanced accuracy on FACED 9-class, +21.5% relative over CBraMod) by ensembling around the universal latent: each checkpoint shares the converged class-mean basin but encodes the within-class valence residual with seed-specific noise; uniform softmax averaging cancels this noise. Our results unify three previously separate phenomena — language-model emotion latents, brain-LLM alignment, and EEG model ensembling — under a single principle.

## V2 — neuro-leaning

A central question in computational neuroscience asks whether the representations learned by language models bear meaningful relation to those used by the human brain. We answer affirmatively for the case of valence, the principal axis of human emotion. From nine short stories — one per emotion category in the FACED 9-class taxonomy — we extract a one-dimensional valence direction that is (i) shared across 18 large language models from 0.5B to 72B parameters, (ii) recovers gold-standard sentiment lexicons (NRC, Warriner) at |r| ≥ 0.76, (iii) predicts the cohort-averaged EEG responses of 123 subjects watching emotional videos at r = 0.87 (p < 10⁻⁹), and (iv) is most strongly encoded in posterior visual cortex (occipital |r| = 0.21 vs frontal 0.16), challenging the dominant frontal-asymmetry account for video-evoked emotion. We further demonstrate that EEG emotion classifiers spontaneously converge to this same valence axis under standard supervision: across 36 checkpoints from two architectures, accuracy correlates with within-class valence-axis encoding strength at r = +0.74 (p = 0.014). Twenty-five attempts to externally inject the axis as an auxiliary loss either fail or harm accuracy above a saturation threshold we locate, providing a clean operational definition of the boundary at which a model "discovers" the axis on its own. We further show that exploiting the within-class valence residual via principled ensembling delivers a new state-of-the-art on FACED 9-class emotion classification (0.6948 balanced accuracy, +21.5% relative over CBraMod), with a mechanistic explanation: averaging cancels seed-specific noise around the universal converged basin.
