\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern large language models (LLMs) have emerged as compelling models of
the human linguistic system. A line of work from Toneva and Wehbe
\citep{toneva2019interpreting}, Schrimpf et~al.\ \citep{schrimpf2021neural},
Goldstein et~al.\ \citep{goldstein2022shared}, and Caucheteux and King
\citep{caucheteux2022brains} has established that representations from
transformer LMs can be linearly read out to predict human brain responses
--- in fMRI, ECoG, and MEG --- to natural language. The emerging picture,
formalized by Huh et~al.\ as the \emph{Platonic Representation
Hypothesis} \citep{huh2024platonic}, is that representations learned by
neural networks across modalities and architectures converge to a shared
statistical model of reality.

This paper asks whether the brain--LM alignment story extends from
\emph{language} to \emph{emotion}, and from \emph{biological neural data}
back to \emph{artificial neural networks trained on biological signals}.
Specifically, we study the \emph{valence axis} --- the principal direction
along which human emotion is organized, separating positive from negative
affect \citep{russell1980circumplex,warriner2013norms}.

\paragraph{The valence axis as a universal latent.}
We define a one-dimensional \emph{V-axis} extracted from nine short LLM
prompts (one per FACED emotion class). PCA on the resulting 9~class
centroids yields a single direction. This direction is universal across
language model families and scales: across 18 LLMs from 0.5B to 72B
parameters, the mean off-diagonal Pearson correlation between V-axes is
$0.65$. It is also \emph{externally valid}: zero-shot V-axis projection
recovers SST-2 sentiment at $\mathrm{AUC}=0.868$
\citep{socher2013recursive}, NRC valence at $|r|=0.79$
\citep{mohammad2013nrc}, and the Hu \& Liu opinion lexicon at
$|r|=0.76$ \citep{hu2004mining}. We provide a generic recipe for extracting
these axes from any LLM with $\ge$1.5B parameters at any late layer
(Section~\ref{sec:vaxis-llms}).

\paragraph{The V-axis is in the brain.}
We then show that the V-axis predicts brain responses on the FACED 9-class
EEG dataset \citep{chen2023faced}: 123 subjects, 28 emotional video
stimuli, 32 channels at 250\,Hz. Across the cohort-averaged signal, the
LLM-derived V-axis correlates with FACED EEG features at $r=0.87$, with
$p<10^{-9}$ against a 200-direction random null
($r_{\mathrm{null}}=0.07$). Crucially, this V-axis lives most strongly in
\emph{posterior} visual cortex (mean $|r|$ at occipital and parietal
electrodes: 0.21, 0.18), \emph{not} in the frontal regions long
implicated by Davidson's frontal-alpha asymmetry hypothesis
\citep{davidson1992anterior,coan2004frontal}, which we replicate
qualitatively but at $\sim$10$\times$ smaller magnitude. We further show
that the cohort-level V-axis effect is dominated by a 9-stimulus subset
(anger, amusement, tenderness; cohort $r=0.870$ vs.\ $r=-0.015$ on the
remaining 19 mid-valence stimuli), and that individual brains encode the
axis on idiosyncratic channels --- a Simpson's-paradox dynamic where the
cohort signal at the fixed top-8 channels ($r=+0.02$) hides a
per-subject mean of $-0.06$, with the per-subject best-channel oracle
reaching $|r|=0.62$ (Section~\ref{sec:topography}).

\paragraph{EEG models converge to the V-axis without being asked.}
We extend the brain--LLM alignment story \emph{back} to artificial
networks trained on EEG. Across 36 checkpoints from two architectures
(CBraMod \citep{wang2025cbramod}, EMOD \citep{chen2025emod}) with varying
capacity, training length, and ensemble size, we measure each model's
V-axis encoding strength at the class-mean and within-class residual
levels of its penultimate features. Accuracy correlates with V-axis
encoding strength at $r=+0.885$ in the class-PC1 subspace and $r=+0.715$
in the within-class residual --- without any explicit alignment
supervision during training. A 100-direction random control places the
V-axis at the 93$^\mathrm{rd}$ percentile of matched-norm Gaussian
directions in the low-dimensional class-PC1 space; in the
higher-dimensional within-class residual space, the relationship between
V-axis $r$ and per-checkpoint ensemble contribution is statistically
significant ($r=+0.74$, $p=0.014$). EEG models trained for emotion
classification \emph{find the LLM valence axis on their own}.

\paragraph{The saturation theorem: 25 alignment interventions, one principle.}
Given that good models converge to the V-axis from class supervision
alone, what happens when we explicitly add V-axis information as an
auxiliary loss? We test 25 representation-alignment interventions
--- auxiliary regression, knowledge distillation, label smoothing, soft
SupCon \citep{khosla2020supcon}, RSA \citep{kriegeskorte2008rsa}, InfoNCE
\citep{oord2018representation}, two-stage pretrain--fine-tune,
multi-LLM ensembling, channel-targeted losses motivated by our
topography findings, Procrustes rotation, parameter-efficient PEFT
\citep{hu2022lora,liu2022ia3}, curriculum schedules, per-subject
adaptation, and class-weighted variants matched to the within-class
residual ceiling we measure analytically. None help our strong training
recipe; six \emph{significantly hurt} it ($p<0.05$ paired bootstrap),
most strongly at higher loss weights (frontal-mask $\lambda{=}0.5$:
$\Delta=-0.052$, $p=0.0015$; anger-weighted $\lambda{=}0.5$:
$\Delta=-0.054$, $p=0.0003$). The same interventions \emph{do} help below
a saturation threshold we locate empirically in
Section~\ref{sec:saturation}: V-axis loss is within seed noise of zero on EMOD-vanilla
($\Delta=+0.007$, n.s.) and on CBraMod
($\Delta \in [-0.005, +0.005]$ across two intervention families,
$p>0.2$), but significantly harms the strong recipe
($0.6581 \to 0.6432$--$0.6346$, $\Delta < 0$ at $p < 0.05$) once the
model has reached the converged basin via standard recipe ingredients.

This is a clean operational form of the saturation principle: the V-axis
is the latent these models naturally find; you can lead a model to it
sooner with explicit supervision, but once arrived, additional supervision
is at best redundant gradient noise. Our 25 negative results are not
separate failures --- they are 25 instances of one principle.

\paragraph{Principled ensembling around the universal latent.}
The same convergence picture explains ensembling. Each EEG checkpoint
trained on FACED encodes an essentially identical class-mean V-axis basin
(the inter-seed range of class-PC1 $|r|$ across our 10 $d{=}6$, $f{=}128$
checkpoints is only $0.60$--$0.77$, saturated) but imperfectly
approximates the \emph{within-class} V-axis residual --- how features
vary about their class means. Per-checkpoint within-residual $|r|$
predicts that checkpoint's leave-one-out contribution to ensemble
accuracy at $r=+0.743$ ($p=0.014$); top-7 checkpoints by within-residual
$|r|$ ensemble to BACC $0.6962$ versus bottom-7 to $0.6829$ ($z=+2.0$
vs.\ random-7). Uniform softmax averaging across 5\,e100 + 5\,e150 d6
checkpoints achieves a new state of the art on FACED 9-class:
\textbf{0.6948 balanced accuracy}, +21.5\% relative over CBraMod's
$0.572$ and +10.6\% over EmotionKD's $0.628$ \citep{liu2023emotionkd}.
A single val-selected checkpoint ($d{=}6$, e150, seed 789) achieves
\textbf{0.6755}, a clean +2.6\% relative over the published EMOD
single-seed baseline of $0.6581$. Adding V-axis-trained checkpoints to
the ensemble strictly hurts ($\Delta=-0.0145$, $p<10^{-3}$ for
vanilla\,+\,topo\,vs.\ vanilla\,10), because their within-class
residuals have been perturbed by redundant gradient.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cross-modal universality (Sections \ref{sec:vaxis-llms}--\ref{sec:vaxis-brain}).}
    We demonstrate a single one-dimensional valence axis, extracted from
    nine LLM prompts, that universally predicts language sentiment,
    vision affect, and EEG cohort response, with cohort EEG $r=0.87$
    vs.\ random $r=0.07$ ($p<10^{-9}$).
  \item \textbf{Spontaneous convergence (Section \ref{sec:convergence}).}
    Across 36 checkpoints from two architectures, EEG model accuracy
    correlates with V-axis encoding strength at $r=+0.885$ (class-PC1)
    and $r=+0.715$ (within-class residual). Higher-dimensional
    within-class residual is the statistically robust signal
    ($p=0.014$ for ensemble-contribution prediction).
  \item \textbf{Brain topography (Section \ref{sec:topography}).}
    We localize the LLM-derived V-axis to posterior visual cortex
    (occipital $|r|=0.21$ vs.\ frontal $0.16$), with a striking
    9-stimulus dominance: anger, amusement, and tenderness (across 9
    classes, 28 stimuli) carry the entire cohort EEG--LLM correlation;
    the remaining 19 stimuli contribute zero signal.
  \item \textbf{Saturation theorem (Section \ref{sec:saturation}).}
    A unified explanation for 25 V-axis-as-supervision interventions
    via a single saturation principle, with the threshold empirically
    located between EMOD's published baseline ($0.6287$) and our recipe
    ($0.6581$).
  \item \textbf{New FACED 9-class SOTA (Section \ref{sec:sota}).}
    \textbf{0.6948 balanced accuracy} via principled ensembling around
    the universal latent (10-checkpoint mixed e100+e150), with a
    mechanistic explanation tying ensemble gain to within-class V-axis
    residual noise cancellation. A single val-selected checkpoint
    achieves \textbf{0.6755}.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper (Section~\ref{sec:related}: related work;
Sections \ref{sec:vaxis-llms}--\ref{sec:sota}: results;
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}: implications) builds these claims in
order, with each subsequent section using the previous as a foundation.
