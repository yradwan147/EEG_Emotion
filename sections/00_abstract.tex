\begin{abstract}
Late-layer concept directions are not free real estate for supervision:
once a network has absorbed a concept from task alone, adding the same
concept as an auxiliary loss is at best a no-op and typically harmful.
We discover and quantify this regularity---which we call
\emph{saturation}---by tracking the principal axis of human emotion,
the \emph{V-axis} of valence, across language models, human brains,
and EEG emotion classifiers. Recovered by PCA on the class-centroids
of nine LLM emotion stories, the V-axis generalises across $14$
language models from 560M to 32B parameters and recovers zero-shot
sentiment from a single Qwen3-4B run. The same axis predicts
cohort-averaged EEG of $123$ subjects watching $28$ emotional videos
at $r{=}0.87$ ($p<10^{-9}$), localised to posterior visual cortex
rather than frontal regions; and it emerges spontaneously in EEG
classifiers trained only on the 9-class label, so LLMs and brain
models converge on the same latent without sharing data. Adding the
V-axis as auxiliary supervision then helps nothing: across $25$
representation-alignment interventions, $16$ produce significant
accuracy decrements, with a sharp transition at FACED-9 BACC
$\in[0.62, 0.66]$. An inference-time directional ablation of the
within-class V-axis residual---the LLM-derived direction projected
out of the classifier's penultimate features---drops accuracy on
every checkpoint at mean $z{\approx}7.7$ above a matched
random-direction null, and per-checkpoint residual strength predicts
ensemble contribution at $r{=}{+}0.74$. Combining the
saturated class-mean basin with seed-specific residual diversity
yields a new FACED 9-class state of the art ($\mathbf{0.6948}$
ensemble, $\mathbf{0.6755}$ single, $+21.5\%$ over CBraMod), and every
claim replicates on SEED-V.
\end{abstract}
