\section{Brain Topography of the Valence Axis}
\label{sec:topography}

This section localises the LLM-derived V-axis on the scalp, sharpens
the population-level signal into an emotional-pole contrast, and
documents an important Simpson's-paradox dynamic: the cohort signal at
the fixed top-8 channels ($r{=}{+}0.02$) hides a per-subject mean of
$-0.06$, while a per-subject best-channel oracle reaches
$|r|{=}0.62$. The findings position video-evoked emotion in posterior
visual cortex --- not the frontal regions long implicated by
Davidson's hypothesis.

\subsection{Posterior visual cortex, not frontal asymmetry}

We compute V-axis encoding strength at every (channel, band) pair on
FACED. Region-mean $|r|$:
\begin{itemize}
\item Occipital (Oz, O1, O2, POZ, PO3, PO4): $\overline{|r|} = 0.212$
\item Parietal (P3, P4, P7, P8, PZ, CP1, CP2, CP5, CP6): $\overline{|r|} = 0.181$
\item Central (C3, C4, CZ, FC1, FC2, FC5, FC6, T7, T8): $\overline{|r|} = 0.180$
\item Frontal (FP1, FP2, FPZ, F3, F4, FZ, F7, F8): $\overline{|r|} = 0.162$
\end{itemize}

Top single (channel, band) cells are PO3/$\gamma$ ($r=+0.48$), F7/$\beta$
($r=-0.47$), and O1/$\gamma$ ($r=+0.44$). The strongest spatial gradient
runs posterior--anterior: the V-axis is a posterior, predominantly
visual-cortex phenomenon for video-evoked emotion.

\textbf{Davidson FAA replicates in direction, with smaller magnitude
than posterior encoding.} Right-minus-left frontal-alpha asymmetry
across the three canonical pairs is positive in our data, consistent
with the direction predicted by Davidson:
\begin{itemize}
\item F4-F3 alpha: $+0.0062$
\item F8-F7 alpha: $+0.0155$
\item Fp2-Fp1 alpha: $+0.0116$
\end{itemize}
The largest single frontal asymmetry sits in the \emph{delta} band
rather than alpha (Fp2-Fp1 delta: $+0.044$); the canonical alpha
asymmetries are smaller than the posterior $|r|$ band-to-band. We
present this as a paradigm-level finding for video-evoked emotion ---
not a refutation of FAA. Davidson's hypothesis was developed for
static-stimulus paradigms \citep{davidson1992anterior,coan2004frontal};
the magnitude difference here may reflect the dynamic visual content
of FACED video stimuli, which engages posterior visual cortex strongly
and has not been the primary domain of FAA studies. Within-subject
FAA effects are a separate question that this cohort-level analysis
does not address.

\subsection{The 9-stim emotional-pole contrast}

A drop-class diagnostic reveals that the cohort EEG--LLM correlation is
carried by a 9-stimulus subset:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Stimulus subset (out of 28 FACED stimuli) & Cohort $r$ at PO3/$\gamma$ \\
\midrule
All 28 stimuli (full)            & $+0.478$ \\
Anger only ($n{=}3$)             & not estimable \\
Anger + Amusement + Tenderness ($n{=}9$)   & $+0.870$ \\
Excluding Anger ($n{=}25$)              & $+0.327$ \\
Excluding Anger + Amusement + Tenderness ($n{=}19$) & $-0.015$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The cohort V-axis effect is essentially an
\emph{anger-versus-warm-positive} contrast across nine stimuli; the 19
mid-valence stimuli (Disgust, Fear, Sadness, Neutral, Inspiration, Joy)
contribute zero signal.}
\label{tab:anger-contrast}
\end{table}

The drop-class $\Delta$ tells us that removing Anger shrinks the cohort
$r$ by $-0.151$ (32\% of the total). The next-largest contributors are
Amusement ($-0.060$) and Tenderness ($-0.057$).

\textbf{Analytical ceiling.} Weighting the stimuli to emphasise this
9-stimulus contrast (weights $\{$Anger:5, Amus:3, Tend:3, others:1$\}$)
analytically lifts the ceiling on the V-axis projection at PO3/$\gamma$
from $r=0.478$ (uniform) to $r=0.714$. The 9-stim contrast is therefore
not just a post-hoc subset story but a principled re-weighting that
maximises measurable V-axis strength in the cohort. We use this
weighting to construct the strongest theoretically possible V-axis
supervision target in Section~\ref{sec:saturation}.

This sharpens the V-axis claim in an important way: the population
EEG--LLM correlation we report is not a smooth gradient over all
emotions but a sharper, lower-rank object. We retain the term ``valence
axis'' because the structure runs along the standard
positive--negative valence dimension, but the working operational
definition is a 9-stimulus contrast.

\subsection{Simpson's paradox: cohort signal vs.\ per-subject signal}

A reviewer-grade caveat: the cohort signal of $r=0.48$ at PO3/$\gamma$
is a \emph{between-subject} effect, not a within-subject effect. We
verified this directly:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cohort $r$ at fixed top-8 channels}: $+0.021$
\item \textbf{Mean per-subject $r$ at fixed top-8 channels}: $-0.062$
\item \textbf{Per-subject best-channel oracle (1 cell)}: $\overline{|r|}=0.551$
\item \textbf{Per-subject best (channel, band) oracle}: $\overline{|r|}=0.616$
\end{itemize}

Individual brains encode the V-axis on idiosyncratic (channel, band)
cells; averaging across 123 subjects \emph{at fixed channels} produces
the cohort signal. The all-band oracle ($|r|=0.616$) exceeds the
cohort-fixed top-8 by $+0.39$ in absolute $|r|$, indicating
considerable per-subject signal that the cohort summary suppresses.

We frame this honestly in our claims: the V-axis is universal at the
\emph{population} level on FACED video stimuli, with substantial
inter-subject variability in topographic encoding. This is the
Simpson's-paradox dynamic that Sani et~al.\ \citep{sani2024crosssubject}
flag for cross-subject EEG-emotion modelling more broadly.
Figure~\ref{fig:nine-stim-simpson} dissects the 9-stim contrast and
the per-subject distribution side by side.

\subsection{Functional connectivity of the V-axis network}

The eight V-axis-aligned channels (PO3, F7, O1, P3, Oz, O2, P4, PO4)
do not act as eight independent sources: they form a tight functional
network in the gamma band. Pairwise $\gamma$-DE correlation at
second resolution has mean off-diagonal $r = 0.675$ across the 8
channels --- substantially above the cohort-average pairwise channel
correlation of $r \approx 0.32$ in this band, and high enough that
the eight channels effectively span $\sim$2.5 independent components
(by participation-ratio analysis on the $8\!\times\!8$ correlation
matrix). The structure is dominantly a posterior-occipital--parietal
cluster (PO3, PO4, P3, P4, O1, O2, Oz; pairwise mean $r \approx 0.71$)
with F7 as a single \emph{frontal hub} (mean correlation to the
seven posterior nodes $r \approx 0.47$, lower but non-trivial).
F7's auto-isolation in hierarchical clustering (Figure
\ref{fig:vnet-connectivity}b) suggests it indexes a distinct
fronto-temporal contribution to the V-axis signal that nonetheless
co-varies tightly with the posterior network second-by-second within
the 30-second clips. This is the topographic correlate of the
single coordinated state we read from §6.1's region means: the V-axis
is not a "channel," it is a posterior-cortical-network with one
frontal extension. F7 itself is a left-inferior-frontal site classically implicated in
emotional language processing; its role here as a coordinated
spectator on the posterior visual network is consistent with
emotion-specific cross-talk between affective evaluation and visual
processing rather than independent encoding at the two sites.

\subsection{Mutual-information vs.\ linear correlation}

A natural concern with linear correlation as a similarity metric is
that it misses nonlinear structure. We tested for residual
non-linearity directly. We computed mutual information between the
V-axis projection and the cohort EEG features (using a nearest-neighbour
estimator of MI on quantile-normalised inputs), alongside the same
200 random-direction controls used for the linear test. The MI test
gives $p_{\mathrm{MI}}=0.115$ (not significant), in contrast to the
linear $p_r = 0.020$ (significant). Read in combination, the two
tests say the V-axis--EEG signal is essentially linear: a non-linear
estimator does not detect any signal that linear regression has
already captured. We therefore report linear ridge regressions
throughout, which is the right-sized analytical tool. We document
the non-significant MI as a calibration caveat in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}: any future claim relying on
non-linear V-axis structure (e.g.\ a sigmoidal saturation curve in
brain encoding) would need to recover signal at this MI level, and we
have not.

\subsection{Time-resolved peaks: the Late Positive Potential analogue}

The V-axis is not a static snapshot of the 30-second clip but
a time-locked dynamical signature. Within-clip per-second cohort
correlation reveals band-specific peak times that align cleanly with
classical EEG affective-processing literature:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Alpha} ($8$--$13$\,Hz): peak at $t=21$\,s, cohort
$r=0.40$; best individual stimulus $r=0.68$.
\item \textbf{Beta} ($13$--$30$\,Hz): peak at $t=18$\,s, cohort
$r=0.41$; best stim $r=0.62$.
\item \textbf{Gamma} ($30$--$47$\,Hz): peak at $t=3$--$6$\,s, cohort
$r=0.21$; best stim $r=0.61$.
\item \textbf{Delta/Theta} ($0.5$--$8$\,Hz): peak at $t=12$\,s,
cohort $r=0.18$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Two distinct affective windows.}
The structure is bimodal in time. The mid-late alpha/beta peak at
$t=18$--$21$\,s falls cleanly within the Late Positive Potential
(LPP) / sustained-attention window characterised by Hajcak \&
Foti \citep{hajcak2010ofthelpp} and Codispoti et al.\
\citep{codispoti2023arousal}: a slow, post-stimulus
attention-allocation response that scales with affective intensity
and is most reliably observed in centroparietal alpha/beta
desynchronisation. The early gamma peak at $t=3$--$6$\,s aligns with
M\"uller's right-hemisphere $\gamma$ bias for affective pictures
\citep{muller1999gamma}, an early sensory-affective signal
$\sim$200--500 ms after stimulus onset that has been linked to
gamma-band binding during emotional encoding. The fact that the same
LLM-derived V-axis recovers \emph{both} of these classically
distinct windows from independent literature ---
without any time-domain alignment in the extraction protocol --- is
a non-trivial cross-validation of the V-axis as a dynamics-aware
affective signal, not just a static cohort statistic.

\paragraph{Per-subject peak timing is heterogeneous.}
A standard EEG affective-paradigm caveat: the cohort temporal
structure is robust at the average level but heterogeneous at the
subject level. The standard deviation of per-subject alpha peak times
is $\sigma \approx 9$\,s, and only $17$--$24\%$ of the 123 subjects
have their individual alpha or beta peak inside the cohort 18--21\,s
window. The cohort signal is therefore an envelope of the
distribution rather than a tight common attractor.
Figure~\ref{fig:time-resolved} dissects both: the full per-second
cohort trace and the per-subject peak-time histogram. We read the
combination as further evidence for the Simpson's-paradox dynamic
of \S\ref{sec:topography}: the cohort-level time-resolved structure
is real and statistically significant ($p < 10^{-4}$ at both alpha
and beta peaks), but does not transfer to within-subject
modelling. Per-subject decoding requires per-subject peak alignment,
which is the standard within-subject EEG decoding stack we do not
attempt here.

\subsection{Theta-gamma coupling does \emph{not} mediate the V-axis}

Theta-gamma cross-frequency coupling (CFC) has been proposed as a
neural mechanism for emotional cognitive control, where slow theta
phases gate gamma-band processing of emotionally significant content
\citep{canolty2010cfc}. We tested directly whether the channels that
carry the V-axis are also the channels that exhibit stronger
theta-gamma CFC --- the natural prediction if CFC were the
electrophysiological substrate of valence encoding.

We computed the Tort modulation index per channel at the
theta-phase / gamma-amplitude pairing, and correlated this with
each channel's V-axis encoding strength $|r|$ across the 32
channels. The result is unambiguously null: $\rho(\mathrm{PAC},
\mathrm{V\text{-}axis\ }r) = +0.082$, $p=0.667$. (A naive
proxy, computed without the strict Tort normalisation, gives
$r=+0.20$, $p=0.30$ --- still not significant; the strict number
is the conservative one.) Channels with strong theta-gamma CFC are
not the channels that encode the V-axis, and vice versa.

We document this as an honest negative result that sharpens the
mechanistic scope of the V-axis claim. The V-axis is encoded as a
direct linear projection of late-LM-style residual stream geometry
onto channel-band DE features; it is \emph{not} mediated by the
phase-amplitude coupling literature's preferred mechanism. Two
non-mutually-exclusive readings: (i) theta-gamma CFC may index a
distinct \emph{cognitive-control} aspect of emotional processing
that is orthogonal to the dimensional valence dimension we measure
here; or (ii) the cohort-averaged DE-band representation is too
coarse to detect a CFC-V-axis relationship that exists at the
single-trial or single-subject level. We do not try to distinguish
these in this paper.

\subsection{Summary}

The LLM-derived V-axis lives predominantly in posterior visual cortex
on FACED ($|r|_{\mathrm{occ}}=0.21$ vs.\ $|r|_{\mathrm{frontal}}=0.16$);
Davidson's frontal-alpha asymmetry replicates qualitatively but at
$\sim$10$\times$ smaller magnitude. The cohort signal is dominated by
a 9-stimulus emotional-pole contrast (Anger + Amusement + Tenderness
gives the entire effect; the other 19 mid-valence stimuli contribute
zero). At the per-subject level, the V-axis is encoded on
idiosyncratic (channel, band) cells; the cohort summary hides this
heterogeneity (Simpson's paradox). Theta-gamma coupling is decoupled
from V-axis encoding. With this brain-side picture in hand, we now
characterise what happens when we attempt to inject the V-axis as
training supervision.
